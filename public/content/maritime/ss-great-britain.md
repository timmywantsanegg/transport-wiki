## Overview

The SS Great Britain was a passenger steamship designed by Isambard Kingdom Brunel for the Great Western Steamship Company. She was the world's first iron-hulled, screw-propelled ship to cross an ocean,  a combination of innovations that made her the most technologically advanced vessel of her era.

When launched in 1843 she was by far the largest ship in the world, at almost 100 metres she was over 30 metres longer than her nearest rival.  The hull was constructed of riveted iron and measured 322 feet in length by 48 feet in the beam, with a tonnage of 2936 gross. 

## Design and engineering

Brunel's design broke with convention at almost every stage. The compartmented hull, an unprecedented 1,500-horsepower engine with chain drive, a balanced rudder, an electric log, a double bottom, and water-tight bulkheads  were all incorporated into the build. Perhaps most daring of all, Brunel rejected conventional paddle wheels in favour of a screw propeller. 

The iron hull gave significantly greater structural strength than timber construction, since wooden hulls were limited to roughly 300 feet before flexing under wave action became too severe.  As Brunel revised his designs, the ship grew larger with each draft, eventually settling at a scale no shipbuilder had previously attempted.

The ship was originally fitted out for the luxury transatlantic trade, designed to carry 252 first and second class passengers with a crew of 130. 

## Service history

### Transatlantic liner (1845–1846)

Her maiden voyage to America began on 26 July 1845, covering 3,100 miles in 14 days and 21 hours.  The crossing proved her technical capability, but passenger numbers fell well short of expectations and the route struggled commercially.

Her career in the transatlantic trade was cut short after she ran aground on the sands of Dundrum Bay in Northern Ireland in 1846, badly damaging her engines.  She was not refloated and towed back to Liverpool until August of the following year. 

### Australian emigrant ship (1852–1876)

Sold to Gibbs Bright and Co, the ship was rebuilt as an emigrant carrier to take advantage of increased emigration during the Australian gold rush.  With a new upper deck and engine, she could transport 750 passengers across three classes, and over the following 24 years and 32 voyages carried more than 16,000 emigrants to Australia,  earning her the nickname the "Greyhound of the Seas" for her speed and reliability.

### Troopship and cargo vessel

In 1854 she was refitted as a troopship for the Crimean War, and again in 1857 carried reinforcements to Bombay during the Indian Mutiny.  She later carried British and Confederate cargo before being converted for bulk freight.

From 1882 she transported coal and wheat between England and the west coast of America, a route that exposed her to some of the harshest sea conditions in the world rounding Cape Horn. 

### Decline and abandonment

In 1886, storms off Cape Horn badly damaged the ship, forcing her captain to seek shelter in the Falkland Islands.  Her owners judged repairs too costly, and she was sold to the Falkland Islands Company to serve as a floating store.  By 1933 she was deemed unsafe even for that role, and in 1936 was towed to Sparrow Cove and left to deteriorate, visited only by penguins and picnickers. 

## Salvage and preservation

In 1967, naval architect Dr Ewan Corlett wrote to The Times arguing the ship deserved rescue and preservation.  The campaign was funded largely by businessman and philanthropist Sir Jack Hayward,  who gave £150,000 towards the rescue. 

On 7 April 1970, divers refloated the ship after days spent plugging holes and pumping out water.  She then began an 87-day, 8,000-mile voyage back to Bristol under tow — the longest tow ever attempted,  finally returning to the same Bristol dry dock in which she had been built 127 years earlier. 

By 1998, survey work found the hull continuing to corrode in Bristol's humid climate, with an estimated 25 years before serious structural loss.  Conservation engineers installed a glass plate across the dry dock at the ship's waterline, with dehumidifiers keeping the space beneath at 20% relative humidity  — a solution that effectively created a dry, climate-controlled environment around the lower hull.

## Legacy

The SS Great Britain is now part of the National Historic Fleet and operates as a museum ship in Bristol Harbour, drawing over 131,000 visitors in 2025.  She was named a Historic Mechanical Engineering Landmark by the American Society of Mechanical Engineers in 1984  and won the Gulbenkian Prize for Museum of the Year in 2006. 

She is widely regarded as the direct ancestor of the modern ocean liner, having proved that large iron-hulled, screw-propelled ships were not only possible but commercially and structurally superior to wooden and paddle-driven vessels.
